# Framing Codebook

Source: reconstructed from Appendix D and Section 4.4.4 of the submitted
thesis appendices.

## Quick Reference

1. `F1` = Economic Pragmatism
2. `F2` = Sovereignty & Debt
3. `F3` = Social & Identity Friction
4. `F4` = Governance & Environmental Justice
5. `F5` = Geopolitics & Alignment

## Core Rule

Assign exactly one dominant frame per article. Use the frame that best captures
the article's organizing logic, even if secondary themes are also present.

The coding workflow used a dominance rule intentionally. Many articles mix
economic, political, and social themes, but multi-label assignment was avoided
to preserve a clear article-level measure.

## Full Frame Definitions

| Frame ID | Short label | Definition | Core problem/logic | Typical cues |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `F1` | Economic Pragmatism | Frames Chinese investment mainly as an economic opportunity that helps Indonesia achieve development goals. | The article treats Chinese DF or FDI primarily as a practical tool for growth, jobs, connectivity, industrial upgrading, or technology transfer, with risks mentioned only as secondary. | Infrastructure delivery, job creation, regional development, competitiveness, industrial policy, "win-win cooperation," pragmatic partnership, project success in terms of efficiency or output. |
| `F2` | Sovereignty & Debt | Frames Chinese investment as a threat or challenge to Indonesia's autonomy, security, or fiscal independence. | The article defines the main problem as loss of control, over-dependence on Beijing, debt sustainability, or strategic vulnerability, including security concerns tied to critical assets. | Debt-trap narratives, APBN guarantees, collateralisation of assets, unequal bargaining power, foreign domination, loss of policy autonomy, national security concerns tied to ports, rail, or strategic infrastructure. |
| `F3` | Social & Identity Friction | Frames Chinese investment through tensions in society, identity, and everyday life. | The article defines the main problem as social disruption, unequal treatment of workers, displacement of communities, or ethnic and religious anxieties linked to Chinese projects, companies, workers, or Chinese Indonesians. | Foreign workers versus local workers, labor conditions, local protests, xenophobia or prejudice, identity politics around "China" or "Tiongkok," community cohesion, and uneven distribution of benefits. |
| `F4` | Governance & Environmental Justice | Frames Chinese investment as a governance and environmental risk that tests the quality of Indonesian institutions. | The article defines the main problem as corruption, lack of transparency, weak safeguards, environmental damage, land conflicts, or rule-of-law issues surrounding Chinese projects. | AMDAL, deforestation, pollution, land acquisition conflicts, consultation failures, elite bargains, corruption, weak enforcement, environmental harm, and procedural accountability. |
| `F5` | Geopolitics & Alignment | Frames Chinese investment primarily as a strategic move in Indonesia's foreign policy and regional order. | The article defines the main issue as how Chinese DF or FDI affects Indonesia's foreign policy, hedging, or regional balance, often relative to the US, Japan, or other partners. | Belt and Road Initiative, BRICS, hedging, bebas aktif, US-China rivalry, ASEAN centrality, strategic leverage, regional influence, and Indonesia's alignment or bargaining position. |

## Compressed Operational Labels

This is the shorter operational version used in the main methodology chapter:

| Frame ID | Label | Operational definition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `F1` | Economic Pragmatism | Investment described as growth, jobs, connectivity, industrial upgrading, "win-win," competitiveness, or technology transfer. |
| `F2` | Sovereignty & Debt | Emphasis on dependence, debt-trap concerns, loss of autonomy, fiscal exposure, or reduced policy space. |
| `F3` | Social & Identity Friction | Labor conflict, ethnic or identity anxieties, "Tiongkok" identity politics, social resentment, or community tensions. |
| `F4` | Governance & Environmental Justice | Corruption, weak transparency, AMDAL or permitting disputes, land conflict, distributional injustice, or environmental harm. |
| `F5` | Geopolitics & Alignment | Great-power rivalry, hedging, strategic alignment, security concerns, ASEAN centrality, or foreign policy positioning. |

## Boundary Guidance

- `F5` versus `F1` is a known hard boundary. Choose `F5` only when strategic
  positioning, alignment, or regional order is the article's main thrust rather
  than background context.
- `F4` should be used when governance failures, environmental harm, or
  accountability problems structure the evaluation, even if the article also
  mentions jobs or growth.
- `F3` should be reserved for articles where social conflict, labor tensions, or
  identity-linked anxieties are central rather than incidental.
- `F2` should be used when autonomy, debt, fiscal exposure, or strategic loss of
  control is foregrounded as the article's main problem.

## Relation to Narrative Tier

Frames are indicators used to construct narrative tier together with stance.
Perceived state-ness is coded separately and does not enter the tier rule
directly.
